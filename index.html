Environment and Its Components — Terminology

Term Definition
Environment The physical surroundings where a given organism is present at the moment.
Habitat Natural environment; physical surroundings where a given organism exists naturally.
Ecosystem Interactions/inter-relationships between biotic and abiotic factors.
Biome A distinct geographic area with specific physical environment (i.e. abiotic factors).
Niche A functional space occupied by an organism based on its activity (e.g., feeding, reproduction habits). Specialized expertise or role of a species in a given community. Smaller unit than habitat. A niche can change from time to time — not as stable as a habitat. Unique for a given organism. Every organism has a niche. A habitat can have multiple organisms.

Abiotic Factors

Abiotic factors include soil, air, water, temperature, light, and humidity — these shape every biome and ecosystem.

Ecological Hierarchy

Individual → Population → Community → Habitats → Ecosystem → Biome → Biosphere
  • A biome can have many ecosystems.
  • An ecosystem can have many habitats.
  • A habitat can have many niches.

Biodiversity

Biodiversity refers to the variety of life at all levels — from genes to ecosystems. It includes: YeastBacteriaProtozoaFungiPlantsAnimals

3 Types of Biodiversity

Genetic Diversity

Differences in DNA among individuals within a species.

Species Diversity

Variety of species present in a given ecosystem.

Ecosystem Diversity

Variety of habitats, ecosystems, and communities (marine, brackish, freshwater, tropical forest, grassland, desert, tundra).

Biodiversity Hot Spots

Classification Criteria
  • Rich biodiversity
  • Endemic (indigenous) species
  • Existence of significant vulnerability
  • In general, species diversity decreases as we move away from the equator towards the poles.
  • Four of the world's 35 biodiversity hotspots are located in India: Western Ghats, Himalayas, Indo-Burma area, and Sundaland.

Respiration Mechanisms — Aerobic vs Anaerobic

Aerobic Respiration

Occurs in most bacteria and eukaryotic organisms (plants, animals). Proceeds in 3 steps:

  1. Glycolysis: Breakdown of glucose into pyruvate (in cytoplasm); yields 2 ATP.
  2. Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle): Chemical reactions break down pyruvate into CO₂ and high-energy biomolecules like NADH and FADH₂ (Oxidation).
  3. Electron Transport Chain (Oxidative Phosphorylation): Electrons from NADH and FADH₂ are transported across the mitochondrial membrane to produce water and up to 36 ATP (Reduction).
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP (energy) Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy
  • Highly efficient — yields more energy per glucose molecule
  • Respiratory waste: Carbon dioxide and Water

Anaerobic Respiration / Fermentation

Occurs in some bacteria, yeast, and sometimes in eukaryotic organisms. Proceeds in 2 steps:

  1. Glycolysis: Breakdown of glucose into pyruvate; yields 2 ATP.
  2. Fermentation (in absence of O₂):
Type Organisms End Products
Lactic Acid Fermentation Animal cells and some bacteria Lactic acid
Alcoholic Fermentation Yeast, some bacteria, and fungi Ethanol (alcohol) and CO₂
  • Not very efficient — yields less energy per glucose molecule
  • Respiratory/Fermentation waste: Ethanol, CO₂, Lactic acid

Comparison Summary

Feature Anaerobic Both Aerobic
Oxygen No oxygen – Uses oxygen
ATP Low yield Makes ATP High yield
Products Lactic acid / Ethanol Makes CO₂ CO₂ + H₂O
Pathway Uses glycolysis – Citric acid cycle + Oxidative phosphorylation
Efficiency Not very efficient – Highly efficient

Fermentation and Its Applications

Definition & History
  • Fermentation = Respiration of anaerobic organism; used as a survival mechanism.
  • Honey wine was produced in Asia during the Vedic period (~1700–1100 BC).
  • The word "fermentation" comes from Latin fervere = "to boil" — because of bubbles produced when crushed grapes are kept in a closed vessel.
  • In 1700 AD, Antoni van Leeuwenhoek (father of modern microbiology) developed high-quality lenses and observed yeast and bacteria for the first time.
  • Louis Pasteur established that alcoholic fermentation occurs by the action of yeast transforming glucose into ethanol, and that lactic acid fermentation is caused by bacteria.

Applications of CO₂ (Fermentation Waste)

  • Bakery: Grain-flour dough cultured with yeast. Released CO₂ cannot escape the elastic dough and inflates it. Baking in an oven creates air pockets due to CO₂ and ethanol vapors. Trace amounts of alcohol can remain (up to 1.9% ABV).
  • Carbonated beverages: Sugar + Yeast → CO₂ provides carbonation.

Applications of Lactic Acid (Fermentation Waste)

  • Yogurt and Cheese: Lactose (milk sugar) + bacteria → Lactic acid → product formation.

Applications of Ethanol (Fermentation Waste)

  • Beer: Malted barley, wheat or mixture of carbohydrate sources (rice, potatoes, millet) + Yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae)
  • Wine: Grapes + Yeast
  • Whiskey, Brandy, and other concentrated liquors: As yeast continues to metabolize sugar, alcohol accumulates and becomes toxic, eventually killing yeast. Different strains tolerate different alcohol levels (up to 20%). = Distillation of fermented products.
  • Bio-fuel (Ethanol): Flex-fuel = combination of gasoline and ethanol. India's EtOH blending target: 20% by 2025. Ethanol blending in petrol now stands at over 15%.

Manufacturing Processes that Contribute to CO₂ (GHG) Emission

  • Alcoholic beverages
  • Soft drinks (non-alcoholic) — CO₂ added for carbonation
  • Bakery products
  • Dairy (curd/cheese-making) products

Symbiotic Relations and Environmental Pollution Roles

Lichen

  • Among the first living things to grow on a fresh exposed rock.
  • A mutualistic symbiotic relationship between Algae and Fungi:
    • Fungus collects water and nutrients from air.
    • Algae perform photosynthesis.
  • It cannot grow in polluted air → acts as a pollution indicator species.
  • Fungus cannot survive in SO₂ environment.

Rhizobium

  • A gram-negative bacteria with a symbiotic relationship with legumes (pea family).
  • Helps increase soil fertility by providing nitrogen through nitrogen fixation.
  • Lives in root nodules of leguminous plants.

Mycorrhiza

  • A symbiotic relationship between a fungus and a plant root.
  • Important for plant nutrition and soil chemistry.
  • Increases plant growth and root development through increased nutrient uptake — specifically NPK (Nitrogen, Phosphorus, Potassium).

Biodiversity Conservation and Regulatory Species

Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD)

An international legally-binding treaty with three main goals:

  1. Conservation of biodiversity
  2. Sustainable use of biodiversity
  3. Fair and equitable sharing of benefits arising from genetic resources

Adopted the Nagoya Protocol in 2010.

Regulatory/Monitoring Species

  • Indicator Species: Species that signal environmental quality (e.g., Lichen — cannot survive in polluted air with SO₂; signals clean air).
  • Keystone Species: Species that have a disproportionately large effect on their ecosystem relative to their abundance.
  • Foundational/Foundation Species: Species that define an ecosystem's structure (e.g., coral in reef ecosystems, trees in forest).
  • Umbrella Species: Species whose conservation automatically protects many other species sharing the same habitat.

Classification of Pollutants

Primary vs Secondary

Primary: Directly emitted from a source (e.g., SO₂ from factories).
Secondary: Formed in the atmosphere by reactions (e.g., ozone formed from NOₓ + VOCs + sunlight — photochemical smog).

Quantitative vs Qualitative

Quantitative: Natural substances present in excessive amounts (e.g., CO₂, nitrogen).
Qualitative: Synthetic or foreign substances introduced into the environment (e.g., DDT, PCBs).

Biodegradable

Can be broken down by microorganisms. Examples: organic waste, food waste, paper.

Non-Biodegradable

Cannot be decomposed naturally. Examples: plastics, heavy metals, pesticides like DDT, nuclear waste.

Types of Smog

Photochemical Smog (Los Angeles Type)

Formed by reaction of NOₓ and VOCs in sunlight. Produces ozone (O₃), PAN (Peroxyacetyl Nitrate), and other oxidants. Appears brownish. Common in warm, sunny climates.

Sulfurous Smog (London Type)

Formed by SO₂ + particulate matter + moisture in cold, humid conditions. Appears gray. Associated with coal burning. London smog disaster (1952).

Greenhouse Gases (GHGs)

GHGs absorb infrared radiation emitted by Earth's surface and re-emit it, warming the lower atmosphere — the greenhouse effect.

CH₄ (Methane) 28 Livestock, landfills, natural gas
N₂O (Nitrous Oxide) 265 Agriculture, fertilizers
HFCs (Hydrofluorocarbons) ~12,400 Refrigerants, aerosols
PFCs (Perfluorocarbons) ~11,100 Semiconductor industry, aluminium smelting

Other GHGs: O₃, SO₂, H₂O. Breakdown of anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions: CO₂ fossil fuels (~58%), CO₂ deforestation (~16%), Methane (~17%), Nitrous Oxide (~8%), Halocarbons (~1%).

Greenhouse Effect and Atmosphere

  • Solar radiation passes through the clear atmosphere.
  • Most radiation is absorbed by Earth's surface and warms it.
  • Infrared radiation is emitted from Earth's surface.
  • Some passes through atmosphere; some is absorbed and re-emitted by GHG molecules warming the lower atmosphere.
A "Stable" Atmosphere (Temperature Inversion)
  • Gradual temperature decrease with height (humid environment)
  • Minimal vertical convection of air parcels
  • Calm and predictable weather
  • Often associated with layered clouds with little vertical development
  • Prone to temperature inversion
  • Pollutants can accumulate in a stable atmosphere

Lapse Rate

Dry Adiabatic Lapse Rate (DALR): dT/dz = g/cp = 9.8 °C/km Wet Adiabatic Lapse Rate (WALR): ~6 °C/km (< DALR due to latent heat release)

Temperature inversion occurs when temperature increases with altitude (reverse of normal), trapping pollutants near the surface.

Thermal Power Plants (TPPs)

  • Much of the coal being burned today in India is low-grade coal and results in significant air pollution.

Coal TPPs are responsible for a higher share of emissions in the industrial sector:

Rankine Cycle (Steam Power Cycle)

Steam at high pressure (P) and temperature (T) is used to generate mechanical work. The Ideal Rankine Cycle involves: Boiler (heat addition) → Turbine (work output) → Condenser (heat rejection) → Feed Pump (work input).

Key components: Coal Hopper → Pulverizer → Boiler → Turbine → Alternator → Electrostatic Precipitator → Chimney; Ash Hopper (Bottom Ash) + Fly Ash Water Storage.

SOx Emission and Bharat Stage Norms

  • TPPs are the major contributors to SOₓ emission; automobile sector is next.
  • Bharat Stage (BS) emission standards (per European Union norms) laid down in India since 2000 to regulate air pollutants from internal combustion engines.
  • Regulated by CPCB under MoEFCC.
  • In April 2020, India implemented BS-VI norm directly (skipping BS-V):
Stage Key Change
BS I Automakers adopted catalytic converters to convert toxic gases to less harmful emissions
BS II Major change — restrictions for Sulphur to 500 ppm
BS III More restrictions; sulphur content no more than 100 ppm
BS IV Emission levels heavily reduced; allowed no more than 50 ppm of sulphur
BS VI Sulphur level just 10 ppm (a long way from 500 ppm in BS II). Sulphur reduced from 50 ppm (BS IV) to 10 ppm in fuel for gasoline and diesel

De-sulfurization is required to meet these standards.

Biomass Co-Firing

  • Biomass co-firing = using biomass in coal thermal plants as partial fuel replacement.
  • Revised Biomass Policy mandates 5% biomass co-firing in TPPs from FY 2024-25.
  • Co-firing reduces emissions of CO/CO₂; biomass contains significantly less sulfur → reduces SOₓ emissions; reduces acid rain.
  • Significance: effective way to curb emissions from open burning of crop residue; helps cut emissions from combustion of fossil fuels.

Cement and Steel Industries

Cement Industry

  • Cement = a binder used in construction.
  • Also called Portland cement (or Hydraulic cement).
  • Becomes hard (sets) through a chemical reaction between dry ingredients and water → mineral hydrates (hard and water-insoluble).
  • Major chemical feedstocks: Limestone, Silica, Alumina, and Iron oxide.
  • A raw mix of limestone (CaCO₃) and clay is heated in a rotary kiln at ~1400–1450 °C.
Calcination Reaction: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ Clinker Formation: 2CaO + SiO₂ → 2CaO·SiO₂ (C2S) 3CaO + SiO₂ → 3CaO·SiO₂ (C3S) 3CaO + Al₂O₃ → 3CaO·Al₂O₃ (C3A) 4CaO + Al₂O₃ + Fe₂O₃ → 4CaO·Al₂O₃·Fe₂O₃ (C4AF)

Role of Fly Ash and Gypsum: Fly ash reduces the need for clinker (reduces CO₂ emissions). Gypsum retards the hydration of cement to make their mixing and transportation to the site possible. Final product involves: 66% Clinker + 4% Gypsum + 30% Fly Ash (fine grinding).

Steel Industry

  • After fossil fuel-based sources, steel and cement industries are the major CO₂ emitters.
Blast Furnace Iron Making: Fe₂O₃ + 3C →(1123 K)→ 2Fe + 3CO Fe₂O₃ + 3CO →(1123 K)→ 2Fe + 3CO₂

Types of iron: Pig Iron → Cast Iron → Wrought Iron → Steel (through subsequent processing).

Petrochemical Industry

Petrochemical Products

Plastics, Resins, Fibers, Textiles, Elastomers, Rubbers, Solvents, Coatings, Dyes & Pigments, Adhesives, Chemicals, Other Consumer Products.

Building Blocks of Petrochemicals

  • A. Syngas (Synthesis Gas): CH₄ + H₂O → CO + 3H₂ (steam reforming); C + H₂O → CO + H₂ (coal gasification). Feedstock: Methane (C1), Carbon from Natural Gas, Coal.
  • B. Olefins [Ethylene, Propylene, Butylene]: Formed by steam cracking of alkanes and naptha. Feedstock: Alkane (C2–C4), Naptha (C2–C4) from Crude Oil.
  • C. Benzene, Toluene, Xylenes [BTX]: Aromatics from crude oil.

Oil Refineries

Crude oil is separated by fractional distillation into: Liquefied petroleum gas (Propane + Butane, 20°C) → Naptha/chemicals → Petrol (gasoline, 70°C) → Kerosene (paraffin oil, 120°C) → Jet fuel/paraffin for heating (170°C) → Diesel fuel (270°C) → Lubricating oils/waxes/polishes (350°C) → Fuel oil/bitumen (>C25 residue, 600°C).

Pollutants from Fuel and Petrochemical Products

  • Fossil fuels (crude oil, natural gas, coal) — burning releases similar pollutants.
  • Low levels of sulfur in biomass: typically 20 mg/MJ SO₂ (considerably lower than crude oil 140 mg/MJ or coal 900 mg/MJ). Natural gas has very low sulphur content.

Dyeing, Pulp & Paper, and Leather Industries

Dyeing Industries

  • Dyeing typically uses between 100 to 300 liters of water per kilogram of textile material.
  • Many synthetic dyes used in textiles, and even food, can cause harmful health effects, particularly in children.

Classification of Dyes

  • Water Soluble: Direct dye, Reactive dye, Acidic dye, Basic dye, Sol. Vat dye
  • Water Insoluble: Vat dye, Sulphur dye, Disperse dye
  • Further classified into: Azo-Dyes and Non-Azo-Dyes
  • Types: Acidic, Basic, Sulfur, Reactive, Disperse, Vat

Chromophore and Auxochrome

  • Chromophore: The part of a molecule that absorbs UV or visible light (e.g., π electrons). It is the light-absorbing group.
  • Auxochrome: A substituent in a chromophore that alters the λmax and the intensity of the absorption.

Altering the Colour of Dyes

  • Red shift (Bathochromic shift): Due to increase in conjugation by the presence of auxochrome → hyperchromic shift (increased intensity).
  • Blue shift (Hypsochromic shift): Due to decrease in conjugation by the presence of auxochrome → hypochromic shift (decreased intensity).
  • The λmax and ε values increase as the number of conjugated double bonds increases.
  • Example: Acetone (λmax = 274 nm, εmax = 14) vs Methyl vinyl ketone (λmax = 324 nm, εmax = 25) for n→π* transition.

Pulp & Paper Industry

  • Water intensive industry — ~100 L of water is required to produce 1 kg of paper.
  • Common pollutants: Sulfur compounds, Chlorinated compounds, and Organic compounds (e.g., Resins, fatty acids, tannins, dioxins, furans, phenols).

Turbidity

  • A measure of the cloudiness or haziness of water.
  • Caused by: Clay and silt, Microorganisms (algae, bacteria), Organic matter, and industrial solids.
  • Measured by Nephelometer: A beam of light is passed through a turbid liquid; suspended particles scatter the light; intensity of scattered light is proportional to turbidity; detector placed at 90° to the incident beam measures scattered light intensity (most particles scatter at ~90°).

  • Desired limits: For drinking water <1 NTU; treated wastewater effluent: typically higher.

UV-Vis Spectrophotometer and Beer-Lambert Law

Beer's Law

Absorbance of a solution (fractional decrease in the intensity of the beam) is directly proportional to the concentration of the absorbing species.

Lambert's Law

Absorbance of a solution is directly proportional to the path-length.

Beer-Lambert Law (Combined)

A = εcl = log(I₀/I) = −log T Where: A = Absorbance (dimensionless) T = Transmittance = Iₜ/I₀ ε = molar absorptivity (molar absorption coefficient) [M⁻¹cm⁻¹] ε ~10,000 M⁻¹cm⁻¹ → Allowed transition ε < 100 M⁻¹cm⁻¹ → Forbidden transition c = molar concentration of solute [M] l = optical path length (length of sample cell) [cm] I₀ = intensity of light incident upon sample cell I = intensity of light leaving sample cell
Derivation Insight

Total opaque area on slab due to absorbers = σ × N × A × dz. Decrease in intensity: −dIz = [Iz × σ × N × A × dz] / A. Integrating: ln(I/I₀) = −σ × N × b, where b is path length.

How to Use UV-Vis

  • Absorbance A of the solution is related to transmittance and incident intensities: A = log(I₀/I) = εcl = −log T
  • Plot absorbance vs concentration at a specific wavelength (maximum absorption — λmax) to build a calibration curve.
  • The calibration curve is a straight line (Beer's Law); slope = ε·l.

Limitations of Beer-Lambert Law

  • Deviations in absorptivity coefficients at high concentrations (>0.01 M) due to electrostatic interactions between molecules in close proximity.
  • Fluorescence or phosphorescence of the sample.
  • Changes in refractive index at high analyte concentration.
  • Scattering of light due to particulates in the sample.
  • Solution: Decrease solute amount or dilute by adding solvent.

Particle Size Measurement — Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS)

  • DLS measures Brownian motion and relates it to the size of the particles.
  • Smaller particles move faster; larger particles move slower.
  • Illuminating particles with a laser and analyzing the intensity fluctuations in scattered light leads to determination of diffusion coefficient.
  • Light scattered by moving particles carries information about size distribution.
  • The velocity of a particle in a unit electric field is referred to as its electrophoretic mobility, measured by Doppler effect due to change in frequency.
  • Particle size is calculated using the Stokes–Einstein equation:
Dh = kBT / (3πμDt) Where: Dh = hydrodynamic diameter (the goal: particle size!) Dt = translational diffusion coefficient [m²s⁻¹] (found by dynamic light scattering) kB = Boltzmann's constant (known) T = thermodynamic temperature (controlled) μ = dynamic viscosity (known)

Typically uses a He-Ne laser (633 nm). Detector is placed at angle θ to measure scattered light, which fluctuates with time.

Total Carbon, TOC, TIC, COD, DO, and BOD

Carbon Compounds in Water — Hierarchy

TC (Total Carbon) ├── TIC (Total Inorganic Carbon): Bicarbonate, Carbonate ions └── TOC (Total Organic Carbon) └── COD (Chemical Oxygen Demand) └── BOD (Biochemical Oxygen Demand) [Not biologically degradable part is COD-BOD] [Not oxidizable part is TC-COD]

Total Carbon (TC) — Combustion Method

  • Combustion method: sample injection by syringe into high-temperature furnace with platinum or cobalt catalyst → oxidizes all carbon materials to CO₂.
  • CO₂ is captured and measured using a non-dispersive infrared (NDIR) analyzer.
  • Other methods: Wet Oxidation (Persulfate oxidation/NDIR), UV oxidation (titanium oxide catalyst).

TIC (Total Inorganic Carbon)

  • Sample subjected to acidic environment → acid reacts with inorganic carbon (bicarbonate and carbonate ions) → releases CO₂.
  • CO₂ released from bicarbonate and carbonate ions represents the TIC.
  • CO₂ is captured and measured using NDIR analyzer.

TOC (Total Organic Carbon)

TOC = TC − TIC

TOC is a more accurate indication of some of the total organic pollutants than a COD/BOD test.

COD (Chemical Oxygen Demand)

  • Amount of oxygen required to chemically oxidize all organic matter in water.
  • Unpolluted water: <20 mg/L; Severely polluted water: >200 mg/L.
  • Includes non-biodegradable organic matter (unlike BOD).

DO (Dissolved Oxygen) — Winkler Method

  1. Put sample into Winkler bottle.
  2. Add 2 mL MnSO₄ solution and 2 mL alkali-iodida-azida (addition from bottom of bottle).
  3. Close carefully, shake for 1 minute → let precipitate decrease.
  4. Add 12 mL H₂SO₄ 6N through partition of bottle.
  5. Titrate with standard Na₂S₂O₃ 0.025 N solution until near endpoint, add starch solution; endpoint reached when blue colour disappears.

BOD (Biochemical Oxygen Demand)

  • BOD indicates the amount of dissolved oxygen (mg/L) needed in a given incubation time for biological degradation of the organic constituents present in wastewater.
  • Un-polluted water: <2 mg/L; Severely polluted: >8 mg/L.

Standard BOD Test

  • A small volume of WW (5–10 mL) diluted to 300 mL using BOD-free water.
  • Incubated at 20°C, under dark or dim light (to prevent algal growth which alters DO level), for 5 days (generally).
BOD = (DOᵢ − DOf) / Fraction of original sample in BOD bottle = (DOᵢ − DOf) × Dilution factor BOD₅ of wastewater = D.O. consumed by diluted sample × [Vol. diluted sample / Vol. undiluted sewage used]

BOD Kinetics (1st Order)

dLt/dt = −kLt → Lt = L₀e^(−kt) BOD (exerted) = L₀ − Lt = L₀(1 − e^(−kt))

BOD₃ vs BOD₅

  • BOD₃: Oxygen consumed by bacteria in 3 days — quicker assessment, may not capture complete oxygen demand.
  • BOD₅: More commonly used; provides comprehensive picture as most organic matter takes days to decompose fully by bacteria.

Example BOD Calculation

Problem: 5 mL waste in 300 mL bottle, initial DO = 7.8 mg/L, 5-day DO = 4.3 mg/L. D.O. consumed in 5 days = 7.8 − 4.3 = 3.5 mg/L BOD₅ = 3.5 mg/L × (300 mL / 5 mL) = 210 mg/L

Hardness

Sum of concentrations of cations (primarily Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺) which will form solid precipitates after thermal or chemical treatments (reacting with some anions).

Very hard water >180

Ca(HCO₃)₂ → CaCO₃ + H₂O + CO₂ Mg(HCO₃)₂ → Mg(OH)₂ + 2CO₂

2NaC₁₇H₃₃CO₂ + cation²⁺ ⇌ cation²⁺(C₁₇H₃₃CO₂)₂↓ + 2Na⁺ (Soap) (Precipitate)

  • Known concentration of EDTA added to water sample until all Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions are complexed.
  • Indicator: Eriochrome Black T (EBT) of red color.
  • At endpoint: solution turns from red to blue when all hardness ions are bound by EDTA.
  • X mL of 0.01 M EDTA = 1 mg of hardness as CaCO₃

Alkalinity

  • Indicates pH buffering capacity; depends on concentrations of ions that will react to neutralize H⁺.
  • Generally: hydroxide, bicarbonate, and carbonate ions.
  • Constituents of alkalinity in natural water: (1) CO₃²⁻, HCO₃⁻, OH⁻ — natural sources, mineral dissolution; (2) PO₄³⁻, HPO₄²⁻, H₂PO₄ — detergents in wastewater; (3) S²⁻, HS⁻, H₂S — decomposition of organic matter by microbes.
  • Expressed as mg/L of CaCO₃; PL: 50–200 mg/L.
  • Measured by neutralizing with H₂SO₄: x mL of 0.02 N H₂SO₄ = x mg of alkalinity as CaCO₃.

  • Carbonate Hardness ≤ Total Hardness
  • Carbonate Hardness ≤ Alkalinity

BET (Brunauer-Emmett-Teller) Surface Area Analysis

Importance of Surface Area

  • Affects dissolution rates
  • Affects electron/ion current density at electrode interface
  • Affects adsorption capacity
  • Represents surface free energy available for bonding in tabletting and sintering

Importance of Pore Size Distribution

  • Affects diffusion rates
  • Affects molecular sieving properties
  • Affects surface area per unit volume

IUPAC Pore Classification

Pore Type Size
Micropore <2 nm
Mesopore 2–50 nm
Macropore >50 nm

BET Theory — Assumptions

  1. Molecules physically adsorb on solid in layers infinitely
  2. Molecules only interact with adjacent layers
  3. Langmuir theory can be applied to each layer
  4. Enthalpy of adsorption for first layer is constant and greater than second (and higher)
  5. Enthalpy for 2nd and higher layers is same → infinite layer adsorption possible
  6. Homogeneous surface; no lateral interactions; uppermost layer in equilibrium with vapor phase; all surface sites have same adsorption energy

BET Isotherm Equation

qA/QM = βA·CA / [(CSA − CA)(1 + (βA−1)(CA/CSA))] Or: 1/[W(P₀/P − 1)] = 1/WmC + (C−1)/WmC × (P/P₀) θ = cp / [(1 − p/p₀)(p₀ + p(c−1))] Where: W = weight of gas adsorbed; P/P₀ = relative pressure; Φ = p/p₀ Wm = weight of adsorbate as monolayer = QM; C = BET constant

BET Area Determination

SBET = Wm · N · a / MW N = Avogadro number; a = cross-section area of adsorbate molecule m = mass of adsorbent; SBET = Area/weight of adsorbent

Porosity — Pore Radius

Average pore radius rp = 2Vliq / S (cylindrical pore geometry) Vp = π·r̄p² · L; SBET = 2π·r̄p · L Average pore size = 2·r̄p; r̄p = 2Vp / SBET

6 Types of BET Adsorption Isotherms

  • Type I: Concave to P/P₀ axis; n approaches limiting value as P/P₀→1; microporous solids; pores inside micropores once filled leave little external surface for further adsorption.
  • Type II: Unrestricted mono-multilayer adsorption; B indicates complete monolayer coverage; non-porous or macroporous; inflection point near completion of first adsorbed monolayer.
  • Type III: Not common; weak adsorbate-adsorbent interactions; porous adsorbents; wider pore size distribution (100 nm range).
  • Type V: Small adsorbate-absorbent interaction potentials; associated with pores in 1.5–100 nm range.
  • Type VI: Sharpness of peaks depends on system and T; stepwise multilayer adsorption on uniform non-porous surface.

Hysteresis — Ink-Bottle Pore Concept

Smaller pores fill with liquid first; large pores empty first. Smaller pores retain liquid longer. This causes hysteresis between adsorption and desorption curves (pore blocking mistaken for cavitation).

Atomic Absorption Spectroscopy (AAS) and ICP-MS

AAS

  • Used for quantitative determination of metals in liquid samples.
  • Measures free atoms in vapor phase; ppm to ppb levels.
  • Principle: Free atoms absorb light of a specific wavelength characteristic of that element (Beer-Lambert Law).

Components

  • Atomizer: Converts sample into vapor of free atoms. Two types:
    • Flame AAS (simple, widely used, ppm level)
    • Graphite Furnace AAS (very high sensitivity, ppb level)
  • Radiation Source: Emits a range of wavelengths
  • Monochromator: Selects specific wavelength
  • Detector

Applications of AAS

  • Water Analysis (Pb, As, Cd)
  • Pharmaceutical and Food Industry for toxic metals
  • Soil Analysis

Limitations of AAS

  • Single-element analysis — instrument measures only one element at a time in a given run
  • AAS is based on element-specific light absorption; each element absorbs only its own wavelength

ICP-MS (Inductively Coupled Plasma Mass Spectrometry)

  • Highly sensitive analytical technique for qualitative and quantitative determination of trace elements (mainly metals).
  • Detection at ppt (10⁻¹²) level; multi-element analysis.

ICP-MS Components

  1. ICP (Ion Source): Generates ions — Argon gas introduced; RF (radio frequency) coil supplies energy; gas ionizes → plasma formed (6,000–10,000 K; RF Power 1,000–1,500 W). Purpose: Atomization and ionization.
  2. Mass Spectrometer: Separates ions by mass-to-charge ratio (m/z). Mostly quadrupole.

Working Principle (Overall Process)

Sample → Aerosol (through Nebulizer) → Plasma → Ions → Mass Separation → Detection
  • Liquid sample introduced as aerosol droplets.
  • Plasma dries aerosol, dissociates molecules, removes electron → singly-charged ions.
  • Ions directed into mass filtering device (mass spectrometer).
  • Separated by mass-to-charge ratio and detected.

ICP-MS Spectrum

X-axis → m/z; Y-axis → intensity. Element m/z: Na=23, Fe=56, Pb=208.

Sample Preparation Methods

  • Acid Digestion: By HNO₃, HF, HCl, HClO₄
  • Sodium Peroxide Sintered Digestion: Rare earth elements made soluble by sintering with sodium peroxide, leaching with water and acidifying with HNO₃
  • Microwave Digestion: For biological/organic samples; sample digested with high-purity acid or peroxide in closed teflon vessel
  • Laser Ablation

Gas Chromatography (GC) and LC-MS

Gas Chromatography (GC)

  • Separates, identifies, and quantifies volatile and semi-volatile compounds in a mixture.
  • Separation based on partitioning between: Mobile phase (inert carrier gas) and Stationary phase (polymer coated inside column).

Components of GC

  • Carrier Gas System: He, N₂, H₂ — transports sample through column
  • Sample Injection System
  • Capillary Columns (most common): Length 15–100 m; Types: WCOT (Wall Coated Open Tubular) and SCOT (Support Coated)

Coagulation and Flocculation

Key Definitions
  • Coagulation: Destabilization and initial coalescing of colloidal particles.
  • Flocculation: Formation of larger particles (flocs) from smaller particles. In treatment, particles collide with others, stick together to form larger particles.

Chemicals and polymers are added to enhance coagulation and flocculation. Chemicals cause (quickly) coagulation, which then (slowly) flocculates.

Colloidal/Interfacial Forces

  • van der Waals Force: Attractive force between all particles at close range. Acts close to particle surface.
  • EDL (Electrical Double Layer) Force: Repulsive force from charged particle surfaces. In water, colloidal particles are typically negatively charged. A diffuse double layer is created by cations attaching to negatively charged particle (fixed layer) and cations and anions loosely attaching in outer diffuse layer.

Electrical Double Layer and Zeta Potential

  • Particle with negative surface charge attracts positive ions (Stern layer) and then a diffuse layer.
  • Zeta potential: measured at the "slipping plane" (boundary between particle+attached ions and bulk solution). Surface potential > Stern potential > Zeta potential.
  • Stable colloids: |Zeta potential| > ~25–30 mV. At isoelectric point (zeta = 0), colloid is most unstable → coagulates easily.

Mechanisms for Coagulation

  • A. Double Layer Compression: Adding electrolyte to water shrinks the layer of charged ions around the particle. If reduced enough, attractive van der Waals force (acts close to particle) can overcome repulsive electrical force. This occurs at fresh-salt water zone in estuaries.
  • B. Charge Neutralization: Adding positively charged counter ions that adsorb to particle surface → reduces surface charge and repulsion. Also, pH variation changes zeta potential.
  • C. Colloidal Entrapment in Aggregates
Why does Alum (Al₂(SO₄)₃) facilitate coagulation?

Alum reacts with water to form Al(OH)₃, which acts as a floc-forming agent. Al³⁺ neutralizes negative charges on suspended particles (B). These aggregates trap other colloidal particles (C).

Common Coagulants

  • Soluble monometal salts: Ferric chloride, Ferric sulfate, Magnesium chloride, Aluminum sulfate (Alum)
  • Soluble polymetal salts: Polyaluminum chloride, Polyferric chloride, Polyferrous chloride, Polyaluminum ferric chloride, Magnesium chloride, Polyaluminum sulfate
  • Organic polymer (polyelectrolytes): Amino-methyl polyacrylamide, Polyalkylene, Polyamine, Polyethylenimine, Polydiallyldimethylammonium

Ionic Strength Calculation

Ionic Strength (μ) = 1/2 Σ CᵢZᵢ² Example: 0.2 M Na₂SO₄ → Na₂SO₄ → 2Na⁺ + SO₄²⁻ C(Na⁺) = 0.4, C(SO₄²⁻) = 0.2 μ = 1/2[0.4 × 1² + 0.2 × 2²] = 0.6

Flocculation Mechanisms

  1. Brownian motion — important for small particles (<0.5 μm)
  2. Stirring — mechanical stirring strong enough to cause particle collisions but not so strong as to break up particles
  3. Differential settlement — larger, faster particles catch up with smaller, slower particles

Types of Settling

Sedimentation

  • Downward settling of particles.
  • Particles may carry adsorbed chemicals.
  • Occurs in a tank called settling tank or clarifier.
  • Purpose: Sludge removal.

Types of Sedimentation Tanks

  • Batch (Fill and Draw/Quiescent type): Inlet → settling tank → sludge at bottom → effluent outlet.
  • Continuous — Horizontal flow: Inlet Zone → Settling Zone → Outlet Zone. Flat bottom or Hopper bottom.
  • Continuous — Vertical flow: Influent from bottom or top; decanting trough (outflow) at top; sludge pipe at bottom.

Key Parameters

  • Settling velocity (Vs)
  • Overflow velocity (V₀)
  • Flow-through velocity (U)
  • Tank Dimension, Detention time, Sludge removal efficiency

Settling Velocity (Stokes' Law — Laminar Regime)

For Laminar flow (Re < 1): CD = 24/Re (drag due to viscous stress only; no form drag) Vs = gd²(ρp − ρ) / 18η [Stokes' Law for creeping flow] For turbulent flow (Re > 10⁴): CD = 0.4 General settling at equilibrium (W + B + D = 0): Vs = [4/3 × (ρp−ρ)/ρ × gd/CD]^(1/2)

Design of Sedimentation Tank (Horizontal Flow Rectangular)

U = Q/(HW) [Flow-through velocity] V₀ = Overflow velocity Residence time: tR = L/U = LWH/Q Overflow time: tOF = H/V₀ Settling time: ts = H/Vs Design constraint: V₀ ≤ Vs → Q/(LW) ≤ Vs Volume = Q × Retention time = Plan Area × H

Standard Design Parameters

  • Detention period: 3–4 hours
  • Velocity of flow: ≤30 cm/min (horizontal flow)
  • Tank dimensions: L:B = 3:1 to 5:1; L = 30 m (common) up to max 100 m; B = 6–10 m; Circular: diameter ≤60 m, generally 20–40 m
  • Depth: 2.5–5.0 m (generally 3 m)
  • Surface Overflow Rate: 12,000–30,000 L/d per m² tank area
  • Settling velocity: typical 0.5–2.0 m/h

Height of Sedimentation Tank

For designing height, Type II (flocculant) settling is preferred. It ensures formation of large aggregates (flocs) to achieve better sedimentation performance in a reasonable retention time.

Zones: A = zone settling, B = clarified zone, C = transition zone, D = compression zone.

Filtration

Goal: To remove TSS.

  • In uniform medium of spherical particles of diameter d, a particle of diameter 0.15d will pass through pores.
  • 400–800 micron particle size granular bed → can strain particles > 40–80 microns.
  • Engineered filter housing: pore size 5–10 microns.

Size of Suspended Matter (Will Pass Through Filter)

  • Soil: 1–100 μm → most retained
  • Cryptosporidium oocysts: 5 μm
  • Bacteria: 0.3–3 μm → may pass
  • Viruses: 0.005–0.01 μm → will pass through

Flow Through the Filter Bed

  • 1. Darcy Flow (Re ≤ 1 — creeping flow): Viscous flow governed by Darcy's Law: Vf = K × hL/L. (K = hydraulic conductivity; hL = head loss across filter; L = depth of granular media)
  • 2. Forchheimer Flow (1 ≤ Re ≤ 100): Laminar flow influenced by both viscous and inertial forces. Head loss: hL/L = k₁Vf + k₂Vf²

Adsorption and Adsorption Isotherms

  • Adsorption: Chemicals adhere to surface of solid (dominant mechanism in water treatment).
  • Absorption: Chemicals penetrate into solid, forming solid solution.
  • Sorption: Includes both adsorption and absorption.
  • Adsorbent: The adsorbing phase (solid surface).
  • Adsorbate: Chemical being adsorbed.

Why is Adsorption Used in Water Treatment?

To remove organic contaminants: taste- and odor-causing chemicals, synthetic organic chemicals, color-forming organics, some disinfection by-product precursors.

Most bonding is physical, occurring by various mechanisms such as van der Waals force. Some bonding is chemical and generally irreversible.

Popular Adsorbents in Water Treatment

  • Activated Carbon: Known for removing organic compounds, chlorine, and other impurities; cheap; can be derived from natural waste (biochar). PAC (powdered, ~24 μm) suspended in water/wastewater. GAC (granular, 0.6–2.4 mm) in packed beds in pressure tanks.
  • Silica Gel: Highly porous; desiccant; adsorbs moisture and some organic compounds.
  • Zeolites: Crystalline aluminosilicate minerals with well-defined pore structure; effective for heavy metals.
  • Chitosan: Natural polymer (linear polysaccharide); from outer skeleton of shellfish (crab, lobster, shrimp); effective for metals, ions, dyes, phenols, pharmaceuticals, pesticides.
  • Carbon Nanotubes (CNTs) and Graphene (GN): Nanoporous adsorbents; large surface area; used for heavy metal removal.

qA = Kd × CA Kd = partition coefficient [mg/kg ÷ mg/L = L/kg]

Langmuir Isotherm

Assumes monolayer molecular adsorption; physical adsorption is fully reversible.

qA = QM × Kad × CA / (1 + Kad × CA) Where: QM = max adsorbent-phase conc of adsorbate (when all sites ST × Aad are saturated) Kad = Langmuir adsorption constant [L/mg] CA = liquid-phase concentration of adsorbate [mg/L] Linearized: CA/qA = 1/QM × CA + 1/(Kad × QM) Plot CA/qA vs CA → slope = 1/QM; intercept = 1/(Kad × QM)

Extends Langmuir by allowing multilayer adsorption. Assumptions listed in BET section above.

qA/QM = βA·CA / [(CSA − CA)(1 + (βA−1)(CA/CSA))]

Cations: Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, NH₄⁺, Heavy metals (Cu²⁺, Pb²⁺, Cd²⁺, Zn²⁺). Anions: Cl⁻, NO₃⁻, NO₂⁻, SO₄²⁻, PO₄³⁻, F⁻, Br⁻. Methods: Titration, Chromatograph.

Activated Carbon — Production

Why Do We Need Activation?

Raw biomass has pores blocked by tars and organic residues. Activation opens pores and increases surface area dramatically.

Step 1: Carbonization (by Pyrolysis)

Carbonaceous material (wood, coal, coconut shells) is heated in oxygen-starved environment to liberate carbon (carbonization). Biochar feedstocks: animal wastes, food wastes, sewage sludge, industrial wastes, wood chips, agricultural wastes, forestry wastes → Pyrolysis → Bio-oil + Biochar + Biogas.

Step 2: Activation (by Pyrolysis)

Carbonized material is exposed to steam or hot CO₂ to cause pores and fissures to form (activation).

Method of Activation

  • Physical Activation: Treating under N₂/Steam to enhance porosity and surface area.
  • Chemical Activation:
    • Step 1 Impregnation: Biochar soaked/coated in solution containing activator (KOH, H₃PO₄, or ZnCl₂; often at specific ratio biochar:activator = 1:10)
    • Step 2 Thermal Treatment: In inert atmosphere like nitrogen at 400–900°C to enhance porosity and surface area.

Applications of Ac-Biochar: Adsorbent, Fuel, Catalyst, Soil Amendment, Additives.

Ion-Exchange Method

  • Chemical adsorption of ions.
  • Used to adsorb cations and anions in water.
  • Making water ions-free (soften) and extremely reduced TDS.
  • Ion-exchange materials: cross-linked long-chain organic compounds with acidic/alkaline functional groups — Resin.
  • Example: Zeolite (Microporous Aluminosilicate) as a cation exchange resin for Na, K, Ca, Mg.
  • Styrene and Divinyl Benzene (DVB) copolymers supported functional groups as cation and anion exchange resins.

Cation Exchange Resins

Sulfonic acid, carboxylic acid substituted organic compounds. Carries acidic functional groups (−COOH, −HSO₃) capable of exchanging H⁺ ions.

RH⁺ + Na⁺ → RNa⁺ + H⁺ 2RH⁺ + Ca²⁺ → R₂Ca²⁺ + 2H⁺

Anion Exchange Resins

Amine, Quaternary ammonium substituted organic groups. Exchanges OH⁻ ions. Strongly basic anion exchange resin (SBA).

ROH⁻ + Cl⁻ → RCl⁻ + OH⁻ 2ROH⁻ + SO₄²⁻ → R₂SO₄²⁻ + 2OH⁻

Ion-Exchange Process Flow

Raw water → Cation exchange column (removes Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Na⁺ → gives H⁺) → Anion exchange column (removes SO₄²⁻, Cl⁻ → gives OH⁻; H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O) → Demineralised/Deionised water.

Advantages and Limitations

  • Advantage: Demineralised water with hardness <2 ppm; usable for acidic and alkaline water; removes both cations and anions.
  • Limitation: Expensive (high resin and installation cost); efficiency reduced if water is turbid; not suitable if heavy metals present (regeneration of resin not possible).

Reverse Osmosis (RO)

  • Process where water is demineralized or deionized by pushing it under pressure through a semi-permeable Reverse Osmosis Membrane.
  • A type of cross-flow filtration (tangential filtration).
  • Goal: To reduce TDS and ionic conductivity.

RO Process

Feed Water → High-pressure pump → RO Membrane → Permeate Water (low conc. of salts) ↓ Reject Stream (higher concentration)
  • High-pressure pump increases pressure on salt side of RO, forcing water across the semi-permeable membrane.
  • Leaves ~95–99% of dissolved salts behind in the reject stream.

Contaminant Removal

  • RO removes up to 99%+ of dissolved salts (ions), particles, colloids, organics, bacteria, and pyrogens.
  • RO membrane rejects contaminants based on size and charge.

Influencing Factors

  • Influent solute concentration
  • Water Flux Rate
  • Pressure: caused by difference in solute concentration between compartments + externally applied pressure.
  • Required pressure: For fresh/brackish water: 15.5–26 bar; For seawater: 55–81.5 bar.

Advantages

  • Simple, cheap, and reliable process.
  • Removes ionic salts AND non-ionic, colloidal matter and high molecular weight organic matter.
  • High installation cost but low maintenance cost.

Applications

  • Domestic Water Treatment
  • Desalination of brackish water or sea water

Advanced Oxidation Processes (AOPs)

To degrade complex organic compounds. AOPs use oxidative power of reactive radicals for oxidative degradation of pharmaceuticals, pesticides, dyes, aromatic amines, phenols, formaldehyde, surfactants.

  • Hydroxyl (•OH) and superoxide (•O₂⁻) radicals are very common.
  • Extremely strong oxidant (E° ≈ 2.8 V); non-selective; transforms complex organic compounds into simple molecules.
  • AOPs oxidize pollutants → CO₂ + H₂O (mineralization).

AOP #1: Fenton Process

Fe²⁺ + H₂O₂ → Fe³⁺ + •OH + OH⁻ ... (1) Organic compounds + •OH → Oxidized products ... (2)
  • Metal-catalyzed oxidation in presence of hydrogen peroxide.
  • Ferrous ion catalyzes production of hydroxyl radicals (•OH) from H₂O₂.
  • Drawbacks: Requires constant addition of ferrous ions; large deposits of ferric hydroxide sludge.

Photo-Fenton Process

  • Regenerates ferrous ions via photochemical route.
  • UV irradiation in addition to Fenton's reagent (H₂O₂ and Fe²⁺).
  • UV facilitates photo-reduction of Fe³⁺ to Fe²⁺ ions.
Fe³⁺ + H₂O + hν → Fe²⁺ + •OH + H⁺ ... (3) H₂O₂ + hν → •OH + •OH ... (4)

Modified Fenton Processes

  • Sono-Fenton: Utilization of sonication with Fenton process.
  • Sono-Photo-Fenton: UV + sonication + Fenton process.
  • Degradation efficiency: US < US+UV < UV+US+H₂O₂ < UV+US+H₂O₂+Fe²⁺

AOP #2: Ultrasound (US) and US+UV Based Processes

  • Ultrasound (US) irradiation enhances generation of hydroxyl radicals through acoustic cavitation.
  • Acoustic cavitation: Ultrasound energy concentrated in form of micro-bubbles generating local hot spots with very high temperature (>5,000 K) and pressure (>500 atm). These facilitate thermal dissociation of water molecules → •OH, O•, HOO• radicals.
  • US+UV: Combined process for better degradation.

AOP #3: Photocatalysis

  • Heterogeneous AOP utilizing metal oxide semiconductors (TiO₂, ZnO, CdS, ZnS, graphitic g-C₃N₄, Fe₂O₃) to oxidize organic pollutants.
  • Titanium dioxide (TiO₂) is the popular heterogeneous photocatalyst.
  • TiO semiconductor exists in three crystalline structures: Anatase, Rutile, Brookite.
  • Anatase form is most effective for photocatalytic activity: Band-gap of 3.2 eV; absorbs photons in UV range (below 390 nm); longer lifetime of electron-hole pairs.

Photocatalysis Mechanism

TiO₂ + hν → TiO₂(e⁻CB + h⁺VB) h⁺VB + H₂O → •OH + H⁺ e⁻CB + O₂ → •O₂⁻ •O₂⁻ + H⁺ → HOO• → ½H₂O₂ + ½O₂ ½H₂O₂ → •OH •OH + Dye → Oxidized products e⁻ + O₂ + H⁺ → HOO• → OH• + (1/2)O₂

Process Conditions

  • Acidic pH conditions enhance degradation: TiO₂ surface becomes positively charged at pH < 6.5, assisting in attracting negatively charged dyes.
  • Air sparging increases photocatalytic degradation due to dissolved oxygen.
  • Drawback: Separating TiO₂ (or other photocatalysts) slurry particles from treated wastewater is challenging.

Identifying Reactive Radicals

Method 2: Electron Spin Resonance (ESR/EPR)

  • Also called Electron Paramagnetic Resonance (EPR).
  • Spectroscopic technique to detect species with unpaired electrons.
  • Detectable: free radicals (•OH, •O₂⁻).
  • Unpaired electrons behave like tiny magnets → energy levels split in magnetic field (Zeeman splitting).
  • Since radicals are short-lived, spin trapping agents are used: DMPO (5,5-dimethyl-1-pyrroline N-oxide) most common.
  • DMPO-OH signal → hydroxyl radical; DMPO-O₂⁻ signal → superoxide; No signal in dark → light-driven process confirmed.

AOP #4: Ozonation

Oxidation of organic compounds in presence of ozone via two mechanisms:

  • A. Direct oxidation by molecular ozone (O₃): Very slow reaction kinetics; selective (unsaturated hydrocarbons and aromatic compounds with electron-donating groups); favored in acidic (low pH) environments; favored in presence of radical scavengers (HCO₃⁻, CO₃²⁻, acetic acid, tert-butanol, Cl⁻) which inhibit hydroxyl radical generation.
  • B. Indirect/Advanced oxidation by free radicals (•OH, HO₂): Non-selective → oxidizes all organic compounds; very high reaction rate but limited by short lives of hydroxyl radicals; favored in high pH or in presence of radical promoter (H₂O₂); can be promoted by +H₂O₂/HO₂, +OH•, +UV.

Biodegradation and Bioremediation in Water Treatment

Bioremediation (Detailed)

  • Utilizes microbial metabolism to break down contaminants.
  • Microorganisms with diverse strains use enzymes to degrade dye-waste.
  • Either off-site (ex-situ) or on-site (in-situ) mode.
  • Biosorption: Adsorption of contaminants onto live or dead microbial biomass.
  • Bioaccumulation: Accumulation of contaminants in living cells of microbes.
  • Biomagnification: Increase in concentration of toxin as it moves up the food chain.
  • Biodegradation: Decomposition of organic matters using enzymatic response of microorganisms.

Types of Biodegradation

Aerobic, Anaerobic, and Anoxic (NO₃ as electron acceptor).

Advantage: Cost-effectiveness and good removal efficiency. Disadvantage: Very slow process; contaminants must be biodegradable.

Accelerating Biodegradation

  • Biosparging: Injection of air under pressure below water table to increase groundwater oxygen concentrations and enhance biodegradation by naturally occurring bacteria.
  • Biostimulation: Adding nutrients (co-substrate) to stimulate growth of native microbes.
  • Bioaugmentation: Adding microbes and organisms to strengthen bioremediation and allow them to take over and decontaminate the area.
  • Integration of AOPs with biodegradation

Factors Affecting Bioremediation

  • Nutrients: nitrogen and phosphorus enhance growth
  • Moisture: required for microbial activity, but excess reduces oxygen
  • Temperature: optimum 25–45°C for enzyme activity
  • pH: most microbes prefer neutral pH

Theory of Biodegradation

S + X → X' + S' (Substrate + Biomass → New biomass + New substrate) dX/dt = k × f(S) × g(X) X = biomass concentration (g/L); S = substrate concentration (mg/L); t = time (hr)

Monod Model for Bacterial Growth

μ = μmax × S/(S + Ks) [Michaelis-Menten type] dX/dt = μₐᵇᵍ × X = [μmax^bg × (S₀/(Kd + S₀))] × X 1/μ^bg = (Kd/μmax^bg) × (1/S) + 1/μmax^bg (Double reciprocal/Lineweaver-Burk linearization) Where: μmax = maximum specific growth rate [hr⁻¹] Ks = half-saturation constant [mg/L] μₐᵇᵍ = specific bacterial growth rate in log phase

In reality, μ decreases at very high substrate concentration → Substrate Inhibition.

Phases of Bacterial Growth

  1. Lag phase
  2. Accelerating phase (Exponential growth) → Log Phase
  3. Deceleration phase
  4. Stationary phase
  5. Death phase

Bacterial Isolation (Lab Methods)

  • Sample collection (soil/water) → Enrichment in MSM (Mineral Salt Medium) → Serial dilution → Plating (spread/streak) → Pure colony isolation.
  • MSM widely used to isolate pollutant-degrading bacteria. Pollutant added as sole carbon source → only capable bacteria survive → enhances degraders.
  • MSM composition: KH₂PO₄/Na₂HPO₄ (buffer), NH₄Cl (nitrogen source), MgSO₄, CaCl₂ (minerals), Trace elements. MSM provides nutrients but no carbon source.
  • Pure colonies subjected to 16S rRNA gene sequencing for genetic identification.

Activated Sludge Process (ASP)

  • Aerobic microbial degradation.
  • Oxygen supplied via aeration (typical: 1.5–3 mg/L).
  • Microorganisms form flocs (aggregated biomass).
  • Organic matter (measured as BOD/COD) acts as food.
  • Converts dissolved and colloidal organic matter → biomass + CO₂ + H₂O.
Organic matter + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + new cells (biomass)

Process Components

  • Aeration Tank: HRT 4–8 hours; T: 20–35°C. Air bubbles for aeration and agitation.
  • Secondary Clarifier (Settling Tank): Biomass settles out.
  • Sludge Recycling System:
    • Return Activated Sludge (RAS): Recycled back to aeration tank; maintains microbial concentration.
    • Waste Activated Sludge (WAS): Excess biomass removed; sent for sludge treatment (digestion, drying).

Type of ASP: Sequencing Batch Reactor (SBR)

  • Modified ASP in batch mode.
  • All treatment steps occur in a single tank but in time-sequenced stages: Fill → React (Aeration) → Settle → Decant → Idle.
  • Advantages: No separate secondary clarifier; good for variable flow; compact (single tank).
  • Limitations: Not ideal for very large continuous flows; potential downtime during cycle transitions.

Type of ASP: Membrane Bioreactor (MBR)

  • Combines ASP (biological degradation in aeration tank) + Membrane filtration (solid-liquid separation).
  • Membrane replaces the secondary clarifier.
  • Continuous process. Types: Submerged MBR (membrane inside tank — most common) and Side-stream MBR (external membrane loop).
  • Advantages: Excellent effluent quality; suitable for high continuous flows; better for water reuse.
  • Limitations: High capital and operating cost; membrane fouling issues.

Attached Growth Bioreactors

Moving Bed Bioreactor (MBBR)

  • Uses free-floating plastic carriers to support growth of microorganisms as a biofilm.
  • Combines advantages of activated sludge (suspended growth) and biofilm processes (attached growth).
  • Advantages: Biofilm grows attached to carriers; high biomass concentration → higher efficiency; resistant to shock loads and toxic conditions; no sludge return system required (no loss of active biomass unlike ASP).
  • Drawback: Biofilm detachment may cause fluctuations.

Trickling Filter (Attached Growth Bioreactor)

  • Wastewater sprayed (trickled) over filter media.
  • Microorganisms form a biofilm layer on the media surface.
  • As wastewater passes over biofilm: Organic matter is adsorbed and biodegraded.
  • Air flows through void spaces → supplies oxygen.
  • Treated water and biomass sludge collected at the bottom; recycle pump sends some effluent back.

Physical Methods: Mechanical Filtration — HEPA and ULPA

HEPA (High Efficiency Particulate Air) Filter

  • Removes particulate matters (PM).
  • Removes at least 99.97% of particles of diameter ≥ 0.3 μm.
  • Used in hospitals, sophisticated industries (semiconductor fabrication), homes.
  • Efficiency: η = (1 − Cout/Cin) × 100

Limitations of HEPA

  • High pressure drop → energy consumption
  • Requires periodic replacement
  • Cannot remove gases or particles below 0.3 μm

ULPA (Ultra-Low Penetration Air) Filter

  • Removes ≥ 99.999% of particles as small as 0.1 μm.
  • More efficient than HEPA filters.
  • Used where extremely clean air is required (cleanrooms in semiconductor fabrication — Fab).

Filter Sequence

Pre-filter (10 μm) → Pre-filter (5 μm) → Fine/Bag Filter F7–F9 (1–3 μm) → HEPA Filter H13/H14 (0.3 μm) → ULPA Filter U15–U17 (~0.12 μm)

Applications

  • Horizontal Laminar Flow Hood (semiconductor workspace)
  • Vertical Laminar Flow Hood
  • Air cleaners in hospitals

Bag House Filter

Used in industrial settings for capturing particulate matter. Dust-laden gas passes through fabric bags; particles are collected on bag surface. Periodic shaking or pulse-jet cleaning removes accumulated dust.

Physical Methods: Electrostatic Precipitator (ESP)

  • Removes fine particles from gas streams using electrostatic forces.
  • High efficiency for fine particles; used in industries.

Working Principle — Step-by-Step

  1. Gas Entry: Dust-laden gas enters the ESP chamber.
  2. Ionization (Corona Discharge): High voltage (20–100 kV) applied; gas molecules ionize → produce electrons and ions.
  3. Charging of Particles: Dust particles acquire negative charge.
  4. Migration: Charged particles move toward positive collecting plates.
  5. Collection: Particles deposit on plates; rappers periodically dislodge collected particles → collected in hoppers.

Migration Velocity (Drift Velocity)

w = qE / (6πμr) Where: w = migration velocity q = particle charge E = electric field strength μ = gas viscosity r = particle radius

Collection Efficiency (Deutsch-Anderson Equation)

η = 1 − e^(−wA/Q) Where: η = efficiency w = migration velocity A = plate area Q = gas flow rate Larger plate area → higher efficiency Lower flow rate → better collection Smaller particle size → harder to collect

Advantages

  • Very high efficiency (>99%)
  • Low pressure drop
  • Can handle large gas volumes
  • Works at high temperature

Limitations

  • Requires high voltage
  • High initial cost

Physical Methods: Cyclone Separator

  • Removes particles from gas stream using centrifugal force.
  • Removes coarse particles; low-cost pre-cleaning method.

Key Steps

  1. Gas enters tangentially → creates a rotating vortex.
  2. Particles move outward due to centrifugal force.
  3. Clean gas exits from top; particles collected at bottom in dust hopper.

Separation occurs when: Centrifugal force > Drag force

Cut-off Diameter (d₅₀)

  • Particle size for which 50% collection efficiency is achieved.
  • Smaller d₅₀ → better performance.

Collection Efficiency

  • High for large particles (>10 μm)
  • Low for fine particles (<5 μm)

Factors Affecting Efficiency

  • Gas velocity
  • Particle density
  • Cyclone size

Advantages

  • Simple design
  • Low cost
  • No moving parts
  • Handles high temperature and pressure
  • Low maintenance

Limitations

  • Poor efficiency for very fine particles (<2–5 μm)
  • Pressure drop can be significant
  • Not suitable for sticky particles

Physical Methods: Absorption (Wet Scrubbing) and Adsorption

Absorption — Wet Scrubber

  • Gas dissolved in liquid.
  • Used for SO₂ removal (and HF, HCl, NH₃).
  • Common in industries.
  • Lime or sodium hydroxide solution sprayed into gas stream to absorb acidic gases.

Adsorption — Activated Carbon

  • Pollutants stick to surface of activated carbon.
  • Removes VOCs and odors.
  • High surface area enhances adsorption capacity.

Catalytic Oxidation, UV, and Biological Methods

Catalytic Oxidation

  • Pollutants converted to CO₂ and H₂O through catalytic reaction.
  • Thermal and catalytic methods available.
  • Used for VOC removal.
  • Catalytic converters in vehicles: Pt/Pd/Rh catalysts convert CO, NOₓ, and hydrocarbons to CO₂, N₂, and H₂O.

UV Disinfection

  • UV light at 254 nm disrupts DNA of microorganisms.
  • Used to kill bacteria, viruses in air and water.
  • No chemical addition required; no harmful by-products.

Biological Methods — Biofilter

  • Biofilters use microbes to degrade pollutants.
  • Removes VOCs and odors.
  • Eco-friendly and low cost.
  • Microorganisms grow on a support medium (compost, wood chips) and metabolize organic pollutants as they pass through.
  • Particularly effective for H₂S, ammonia, and other odorous compounds.

Advanced Methods

  • UV disinfection
  • Photocatalysis (TiO₂) — same mechanism as in water treatment
  • Plasma purification — non-thermal plasma generates reactive species (•OH, O•, O₃) that oxidize pollutants

Conclusion

Key Takeaway
  • No single method is sufficient for complete air treatment.
  • Hybrid systems are preferred (e.g., cyclone + ESP + wet scrubber).
  • Future direction: smart and sustainable systems using renewable energy and intelligent monitoring.
